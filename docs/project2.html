Where does the Internet come from?

Most people don't really know where the Internet comes from. In reality, it's not really important to know. Knowing about the Internet's conception and early developments is like knowing the same information about the telegraph, or the television. Sure, they're significant advancements for humanity, but we take them for granted given the conveniences they provide.

For the Internet though, its conception was thanks to computer scientists Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn, whom helped develop it. The Internet was born from another experiment called ARPANET, a Department of Defense research project. ARPANET was the result of developing a communications system that could survive a nuclear attack during the Cold War.

Led by computer networks engineer Paul Baran, the idea behind ARPANET was to create a system that breaks up messages into blocks and sends them as fast as possible in every possible direction through a mesh network, ensuring that the messages would get to their destination even with interruptions in the network. This concept was later used and called "distributed packet-switched network."

With the Internet, no single entity controls the network. It is instead made up of an incredible amount of independently-operated networks, making it fully-distributed with no central control. These networks are all motivated to assure end-to-end conductivity to all devices, creating an unprecedently level of connectivity with people.
