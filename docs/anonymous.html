Becoming Anonymous

If you ever wanted to become anonymous, this guide goes over some of the easiest and most extreme ways an individual can go private on the Internet. For a frame of reference, the average person should at the very least be following the basic measures, with the more concerned person taking the intermediate measures. The advanced and extreme measures are for people whom are passionate about online privacy and want to keep themselves practically off the grid.

Basic Measures

  • Use a password manager and keep all your passwords you use for all the website you visit inside of it. Your passwords need to be strong; strong passwords have a lot of numbers, letters, and symbols. A password manager will generate passwords for you, but you will still need to remember a master password for your password manager.
  • The web browser you use. For a basic measure, you can use whatever browser you want, be it Microsoft Edge, Google Chrome, Opera, or any other web browser. If you install any addons to your browser though, make sure it is a trusted source. Finally, you will want to switch your search engine (the websites like Google or Yahoo!) to something more secure like duckduckgo or startpage.

  • 2-Factor Authentication. This is an additional security measure after you input your password for a website. This is typically in the form of a text message, but lately companies are transitioning to an app called Google Authenticator. The latter is more secure, but either methods will help you secure your accounts.
  • Get a VPN (Virtual Private Network). This software will be able to hide your identity on the services you visit, and no one will be able to accurately track you through your IP address (your digital address). NordVPN is a good option for a VPN service, and costs very little for your total privacy.
  • Minimize your social media usage. This might not be as hard at your age, but you'll want to keep as little information about yourself on your social media profiles as you can. No real names, addresses, phone numbers, et cetera. Don't talk to strangers, don't comment on public posts, don't post pictures of yourself, and for phones, turn off any permissions for things you don't use, like tracking location or SMS text messages.
  • Don't put your phone number out there. If you need to input a phone number when signing up at a website, consider spoofing your phone number that'll redirect any incoming calls to your phone.
  • For your smartphones and computers, disable any settings that use your digital data, disable location services and Bluetooth, and remove any unused or unnecessary applications.

    • Intermediate Measures

  • Used encrypted text messaging. To put it simply, you'll want to use secure services like iMessage (built into your iPhone) or Signal. Signal is fully encrypted, ensuring that you and the person you’re texting are the only people that have access to your conversation. This service also works for phone calls.
  • Secure your hard drive storage. If you didn't know, whenever you delete your files on your computer, they don't actually disappear permanently. Instead, it just opens up more free space that allows new data to overwrite the deleted data. If you want to prevent anyone from accessing your deleted data, you'll want to get a program like CCleaner to clean up your free-space storage. Finally, you'll want to encrypt your hard drive with something like BitLocker, a service included with Windows operating systems. Check this guide here if you want to learn more.

  • Change your email provider to something much more secure. Most email providers invade your privacy in some way, so you'll want to use a different service like ProtonMail, which is open-source and has end-to-end encryption, making it very secure. If you have difficulty with switching with email providers, check this guide here.
  • Get a smartphone with constant security updates. Between Androids and iPhones, the average individual will want an iPhone to be more secure, as most of Apple's phone models get constant security updates, where many Android products do not. Later on though, if you really want to up your online security game, you'll pick an Android product, which we'll go over later in this list.
  • Only use traditional passwords. Traditional passwords in this sense are the ones with letters and numbers and such. Do NOT use any type of other security for your passwords. This means things like fingerprints, face scans, PINs, et cetera. For a bonus, you should also make sure the router you use at home isn't using the default password it comes with.
  • Use a privacy-based browser. You'll want to use new web browsers such as Firefox. Avoid Google Chrome and any other chromium-based browsers as they collect your private data. You would also be better off getting some web extensions that help block online advertisements (Ublock Origin) and prevent tracking (Privacy Badger).

    • Advanced Measures

    From this point onward, these measure may be out of the realm for the average elderly person to care about. Don't worry, but if you're a real enthusiast about online privacy, keep reading!

  • Change your computer's operating system. The following operating system types are ranked from worst to best for security: Windows, OS X, Linux Guest, Linux. Get a Linux-based operating system such as Lubuntu, Linux Mint, Ubuntu, or Fedora. If you're hesitant, try out the operating system on a virtual machine (a computer within your computer) to see if you'll like it.
  • Ditch your current smartphone. One of the alternatives is a basic flip phone that has no SMS feature, as texting on them wouldn't have any encryption. Another is an Android device with a custom privacy-oriented ROM. It's not important to know too much of what that is, but Copperhead OS is a perfect example of one. With your Android device, you'll want no Google services installed (you'll have to download new apps with the F-Droid Store) and you'll want to disable Bluetooth entirely.

  • Ditch Google and social media. This might not be too hard for your age group, but for the rest of the world, this is a very unlikeable step in the process of reaching total online anonymity. Google and social media websites are complete hotbeds for data extraction, and are essentially the antithesis to seeking total online privacy.
  • Password-protect your BIOS. The BIOS of your computer is important for providing services for operating systems and programs. Protecting it will ensure no one can get access to your computer's core systems and modify anything within it.
  • Only use the Tor Browser as your new web browser. It is one of the only web browsers to securely encrypt your traffic and mask your IP address whenever you're visiting a website. It makes great efforts in doing so, unlike any other widely-known web browser. The Tor Browser is available on your computer, but you can look for alternatives on your phone such as Orbot and Orfox for Android devices, and Red Onion for iPhones; Firefox Focus is an alternative too.
  • Spend money online anonymously. There are a couple ways you can do this. One is purchasing pre-paid debit cards (in cash), and activating them with false information. You can use the Tor Browser as mentioned above to anonymize your traffic, so no worries if you're lying. Another method is using cryptocurrency; apps like Coinbase can help you with learning how to do so. The issue with cryptocurrency is that many websites do not support them yet, as they're generally unregulated and not backed by governments.

    • Extreme Measures

  • The only usable smartphone at this stage is one with a privacy-based custom ROM. Other than that, you can get a flip phone.
  • Social-engineer your life. It sounds weird, but when people are looking for information on you, they'll likely look to people close to you or contact you directly. To avoid this, don't talk to strangers, never give out real personal info anywhere (online or in real life), and even go as far as to change your speech patterns. Similar phrases and catchphrases CAN and WILL be tracked.
  • Spoof your MAC address. Your MAC address identifies the "address" of the device you're currently using. If you spoof it, you'll make yourself more anonymous to whatever network you're connecting to. SMAC is a good resource in learning how to do so.

  • Go as far as to erase your entire identity. It goes without saying, but this is the most extreme step you can take. You'll want to undertake a number of things in order to assure your privacy at this stage. First, you'll want to fill your e-mail with mail bombers, filling them up with random junk mail INTENTIONALLY. Next, with all of your accounts, upload random pictures of random people and make up information for them.This makes it hard to determine who the account belongs to and much harder to connect them back to you. Finally, delete as many accounts as possible. If you can't do so with some websites, email the service directly as in most cases they are legally obligated to do so. Overall, the goal with these steps is to ensure your whole online identity is separate from your real identity.

    • In conclusion, these tips are here to outline the safety practices you can take in the goal to reach total online anonymity. Don't be afraid to search your web browser for more information on these topics, as this was just a quick bulleted list of info for each and every measure you can take.
