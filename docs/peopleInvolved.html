People

Connected people

People strive to communicate above all else; before the Internet, people were subjugated to culture through mediums like newspapers or television, mediums that were controlled by large companies. With the revelation of the Internet, there's a new method of communication, allowing people to find information, speak their own, and collaborate with each other through a democratic-like medium.

With the development of the Internet, a term was coined called Web 2.0; it's a term that emphasizes websites who host user-generated content, where users participate in the community through the information they put out themselves and the information they find from other users. The following people are examples of founders of websites of Web 2.0.

Mark Zuckerberg

Co-founded Facebook alongside his Harvard roommates.

Wikipedia

Facebook

Facebook emphasizes the way relationships are mapped out, and how many a single individual can have given the people they're connected to. Zuckerberg believes that his website has the potential to fundamentally change the Internet, creating an exponential growth in his user base. His ambition for the site is to create a service that embodies the totality of human connectedness; in other words, the "ultimate relationship engine."

The website maps out all types of relationships people have into a "social graph" where people are bound together into a web of relationships. For example, if you have a friend on Facebook, and if that friend has another friend on Facebook, then by the power of this "web," you have a relationship with your friend's friend in some manner.

Facebook today is considerably powerful online, being a medium in which friends and families come together to connect online through mutual interests and relationships. So powerful so that Microsoft had invested $240 million into Facebook just for 1.6% shareholding power.

Chad Hurley

Co-founder and former CEO of YouTube.

Wikipedia

YouTube

YouTube is the main online video-sharing platform. The website makes efforts to "change the world" through people-power, connecting indivduals (and making money) through the medium of video. For example, the act of recording and sharing video had once many barriers to entry (mostly related to money), but with YouTube it is now much more accessible with people being able to broadcast themselves through whatever simple equipment they have on-hand. A camcorder is enough to share your favorite childhood moments with the Internet.

YouTube hosts countless videos, including copyrighted contents from television and movies. In an act to hurt the website, companies like Viacom spoke out against them, filing a lawsuit accusing them of "brazen disregard for intellectual property law, because of the copyrighted material that appears on the site." YouTube was able to stand its ground against threats like these though, due to their acquirement by Google back in 2006 providing them lots of funds to do so.

Kevin Rose

Co-founder of Digg along with Jay Adelson.

Wikipedia

Digg

Digg is a unique social news website where the premise is that its content is chosen by its user base. The content that the website's journalists write for it is voted on by the users, whom vote on stories that they want to see.

The uniqueness of it really gives power to the individuals who want to hear specific stories that they themselves voted for instead of the content provided for them by traditionalist mediums. The website was on the same plane as relevance as many popular newspapers at the time, such as the New York Times.

Shawn Fanning

Developer of Napster, the first popular P2P file sharing platform.

Wikipedia

Napster

Napster was a peer-to-peer program dedicated to online music distribution, and it was one of the most popular ones on the Internet at its peak. The way it worked is that as long as someone, somewhere connected to Napster had the MP3 file you were looking for, you could download it off their computer. The program wasn't even officially downloadable at first, distributed manually from Fanning, but its popularity exploded through America, passing over a million downloads after a few months.

There were repercussions for distributing music online though. For one, it's illegal; it is literally the distribution of copyrighted material. Of course, it was happening at a rate of tens of millions of shared files per day, so it would be difficult go after individual users for participating in it. The music industry, recording studios and artists alike, were against Napster during its popularity. For instance, Metallica, the heavy-metal band, threatened to sue the site in 2000, asking them to ban around three-hundred thousand users who engaged in distributing Metallica's music. The lawsuit boosted the site's popularity and hurt Metallica's image. The Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) filed a lawsuit as well, accusing Napster of copyright infringement. The suit's verdict was for the site to be shut down

Despite their shutdown, the supposed effects of online piracy still happened; music sales still declined, and illegal file sharing still happened in great numbers. The site's legacy is how other companies picked up where Napster left off, like Apple's iTunes.

Craig Newmark

Founder of craigslist, the most popular classified-ad website.

Wikipedia

Craigslist

Craigslist allows for users to reequest or offer services and goods in an online "noticeboard" of sorts. It takes inspiration from newspapers, who do "classified ads," but craigslist manages to do that more effectively utilizing the Internet, all for free.

The website itself is living proof of how websites and the Internet around about making money, but settings communications free and giving people power to express themselves.
