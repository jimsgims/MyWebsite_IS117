.Com Bubble

e-Commerce

The internet brought about the age of online shopping, changing how we buy and sell things with websites like Amazon and eBay. Amazon was launched in mid-1995 by founder Jeff Bezos, whom was obsessed with spreadsheets and the idea of selling books online. eBay was launched around the same time by Pierre Omidyar, but he was the complete opposite of Bezos in terms of professionalism; Bezos had an extremely intricate business plan in place backed by market research when starting his website, while Omidyar was simply fascinated behind his idea, putting no further thought into making it than that.

eBay held the belief that an auction site online would be fairer and more accessible than any existing market in the physical world. Initially, the site was boggled down with seemingly useless junk that people were selling, mostly due to its popularity with people whom collected things. Regardless, Omidyar was raking in thousands of dollars worth of fees within months of launching the website. Bezos himself was doing much better, shipping books to over 45 states in the US and to over 50 countries worldwide within a month of launching Amazon. His initial business plan called for slow growth, but was modified quickly due to how quickly users were latching onto the site.

What drives the growth for the Internet?

There's a few things that really exploded how effective and fast the Internet became.

  • Moore's Law is a concept that correctly predicts that technology progress of microprocessors will double in efficiency roughly every two years. This is important for the development of newer, faster computers.
  • Metcalfe's Law states that every new node added to the network increases its efficiency, making it more useful and becomes more compelling for new users to join the network. The more popular that computers and the Internet were becoming, the more powerful it can become.

    • Overall, the Internet was really taking off in the late 1990s, with rampant enthusiasm and unbridled greed taking the wheel and bringing about a bubble of economic growth.

    The Bubble

    To summarize, a "bubble" in American economics have started with risky start-ups and eager investors, and end with many bankruptcies, foreclosures, and stock market immolation. It's happened throughout our history with industries such as railroads, but where it started on the Internet was with Netscape's "Navigator" browser. Following it were the several search engines like Yahoo! and Excite!, and online stores like Amazon. For Amazon, their initial public offering in mid-1997 was somewhat dubious on the surface. The company wasn't very old at the time (about two years), there were precious few revenues, no profits.

    Bezos made it an effort to hype up the company as much as possible though. His successes for Amazon come from his business plan of foregoing short-term profits and revenue in exchange for rapid growth. Bezos coined his plan with a motto: "Get big fast." The rapid growth leads to an obvious increased scale for operation, and customers will trust the company for their effective service.

    Speaking of trust, Amazon had the problem of how they were going to get customers' trust in giving them their credit card information for online purchases. They wanted a near-perfect way to secure their info and ensure they aren't picked up by hackers. The result was public key encryption, developed by mathematicians Whitfield Diffie, Martin Hellman, and Ralph Merkle. An explanation of how it works is on the right.

    Back to eBay, their initial business model was hazy, collectors flooding the site with odd trinkets, making it unprofessional and reminiscent of a flea market. The company wanted to be taken serious, so they hired Meg Whitman, a marketer at Walt Disney and an executive at Hasbro. With her assistance, the initial public offering in late 1998 soared the first day.

    Wall Street Frenzy

    The trends with eBay and Amazon having crazy initial stock prizes wasn't uncommon during the .com bubble. People were in a frenzy purchasing stocks, with crazy investments being made into any company based on the Internet. Companies with no real business plan, poor purposes, or lacking profits still got investments. The successes of Amazon and eBay inspired thousands to imitate them and take a shot at the Internet business. It was practically the new-age Gold Rush. These prospective businesspeople focused less on profits and more on the potential money that their company's stock could generate from Wall Street.

    Furthermore, the stock market was becoming more democratized; everyday people were getting more opportunities to participate in stocks through mediums like day traders. This mass intake of new participants in the stock market helped grow the fervent investments of crazy amounts of money into websites, all without the thought of consequences. People were giving this as much thought as if they were gambling, and at the time, they weren't too different from each other. This, in short, is what caused the .com bubble.

    Growth was so great for the Internet that fiber networks were being built across the country. These fiber networks were necessary so that computer networks could easily connect with one another. Because of this bubble, there was heightened interest to create these fiber networks for better connections. While not necessarily a bad thing, it's noteworthy that the companies layout out fiber networks were overdoing it; too many companies were doing the labor, there was a lot of overspending, and there wasn't much thought for costs or consequences.

    The Popping of the Bubble

    Eventually, intervention was taken to try and pop this bubble. Federal Reserve head chairman Alan Greenspan, was initially favorable towards the bubble, stating that the technology behind the Internet was creating a new economy that is unlike anything we've experienced before. He did become more fearful of it though, fearing a total economic collapse, and by early 2000 he enacted higher federal interest rates in an effort to end the bubble.

    The bust on April 14th, 2000 was called "Black Friday." The NASDAQ fell 355 points in one week, making it one of the single greatest collapses in the stock market. It ended up losing two-thirds of its value from its $5000 peak. All in all, about $3.5 trillion was lost within a year.

    Black Friday was the conclusive end of the .com bubble. Many tech giants were wounded, but many more smaller websites went kaput. Wall Street was blamed for the over-hyping of companies, with New York Attorney General Elliot Spitzer leading prosecution against a few Wall Street executives over the ordeal.

    There were positives to the end of this bubble, though:

  • Important technological progress was made with the websites whom survived the crash, and we now have companies whom benefit the economy with their new practices.
  • Helped empower regular people by giving them tools to what they want to achieve online.
  • As stated by Andy Grove, president of Intel, untold sums of billions of dollars was spent in building up the Internet's infrastructure, such as the fiber networks mentioned above. These benefits may have come eventually, he mentioned, but the bubble made them happen in five years rather than fifteen.
  • Brick-and-mortar companies were practically forced to move online and become more involved with the Internet in order to stay afloat and compete.

    • To conclude, the bubble and eventual crash shows the progression of how the Internet has developed for a worldwide economy, and how it’s revolutionizing it so efficiently and quickly. There were losers, but we all came out winners with the progress we made along the way.
