Domain Names and ICANN

What is ICANN?

ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) is a nonprofit organization responsible for coordinating the maintenance and procedures of several databases related to the namespaces and numerical spaces on the Internet. In layman's terms, they're an important company dedicated to running and coordinating connections for websites.

Most of their work focuses on the following:

  • Policy development for internationalization of the domain name system
  • Introduction of new generic top-level domains (TLDs) such as .com, .gov, and more
  • Operation of root-name servers (essentially top-level DNS servers)

    • History of ICANN

    Before ICANN was established, administrating the registering of IP identifiers was performed by a single man named Jon Postel, a computer science researcher involved in the creation of ARPANET. He was solely in charge of administering the Internet Assigned Numbers Authority (IANA), an informal sect of ARPANET. Postel mentioned in a 1997 testimony to Congress that this work had come about as a "side task," to his research. He worked on this until his death in October of 1998, to which his colleague Joyce K. Reynolds helped transition his work to what ICANN would become.

    A photo of Jon Postel, administrator of IANA

    As the Internet grew, the U.S. Department of Commerce initiated a process to establish a new organization to perform Postel's tasks. The result was ICANN, officially incorporating on September 30th, 1998, being led by founding chairwoman Esther Dyson. It was designed to privatize the management of Internet names an addresses in a manner that allows open development of competition, facilitating global participation in Internet management.

    Domain Names

    ICANN is responsible for the development, expansion and evolution of the domain name system. They oversee the huge and interconnected network of unique identifiers that allow computers on the Internet to find one another.

    They're also tasked with drawing up contracts with domain registrars, ensuring that these registries provide adequate and consistent service. There's a company in charge of every top-level domain, managing the domains and IP addresses associated with them. There are also registrars who sell these domains, whom are technically free to charge whatever they still have to pay a set domain fee to the particular registry they utilize. The contracts ICANN creates ensures a consistent and stable environment for the domain name system.

    Finally, they coordinate how IP addresses are created. In a similar fashion to how the DNS is used by humans, they play the administrative role in how IP addresses are used by computers. They don't necessarily run the system, but they help coordinate how IP addresses are supplied to avoid repetition or clashes.
